Somewhere in your organization, the decision about how employees will work with AI agents has probably already been made, and it was made by whichever tool the most enthusiastic early adopter installed first. That is a poor way to select the layer of the stack your people will touch every working day, in a market where major products are rebranded, deprecated, or replaced on a timescale of months.

In [Intelligent Business: A Modular Approach to AI Integration](/papers/intelligent-business) I call this layer the **agentic harness**, and the whitepaper's advice is deliberately brief. Select for model and inference hosting flexibility and for a user-friendly interface. This article expands that advice into working selection criteria, because the harness decision is one your organization will revisit, and the criteria need to outlive whatever product wins the current quarter.

## What a harness actually is

A harness equips an agent with a preconfigured, role-specific toolbelt. The analogy deserves the extra sentence it takes to develop. An electrician and a carpenter both carry toolbelts, but the contents differ, the contents are chosen before the job starts, and nobody hands an apprentice the master's belt on day one. A harness does the same work for an agent and its operator. It determines which tools the agent can reach, which credentials it carries, which model it reasons with, and what the human sees while the work happens.

Harnesses span a spectrum. At one end sit command-line tools running in a terminal, favored by engineers who want the agent inside their existing workflow. At the other end sit full workstation applications, including agent-integrated development environments and desktop apps, that wrap the same capabilities in a graphical interface suitable for analysts, operations staff, and anyone else who does not live in a shell. In the architecture the whitepaper describes, this is Layer 1, the agentic workstation where requests originate and where every action first gets tied to a named human operator.

The mistake to avoid is treating the harness as an application choice with application-sized consequences. It is where your workforce's daily AI habits form, where role-based permissions are either enforced or quietly bypassed, and where a vendor's roadmap decisions land directly on your people.

## The landscape, mid-2026

A brief survey of the current market makes the case for criteria over brand loyalty better than any argument could.

On the terminal end, Anthropic's Claude Code and OpenAI's Codex CLI are the most widely deployed vendor harnesses. They differ instructively. Claude Code is a proprietary harness tied to Anthropic's own models, with a mature ecosystem of skills, subagents, and lifecycle hooks. Codex CLI is [open source under Apache 2.0](https://github.com/openai/codex), defaults to OpenAI models but officially supports running against local models, and sandboxes shell execution by default. Vendor-neutral alternatives such as [OpenCode](https://opencode.ai/) take flexibility furthest, supporting dozens of model providers with bring-your-own-key economics. On the workstation end, agent-integrated editors and desktop platforms offer the same underlying loop behind interfaces that non-engineers will actually use.

The cautionary tale is Google. In May 2026 the company announced it was [retiring Gemini CLI for individual and consumer tiers](https://developers.googleblog.com/an-important-update-transitioning-gemini-cli-to-antigravity-cli/) in favor of the closed-source Antigravity CLI, and on June 18 the old tool simply stopped serving requests for those accounts, with no grace period and without full feature parity in the replacement. Enterprise license holders were spared, but any team that had built scripts and habits around the consumer tool got roughly thirty days to migrate. Gemini CLI was open source, widely starred, and backed by one of the largest companies on earth. None of that protected the workflow built on top of it.

That is the environment you are procuring in. Products at every point on the spectrum are genuinely good, and any of them may be unrecognizable in eighteen months. So the durable question is what to demand of whichever one you choose.

## Criterion one: model and inference hosting flexibility

The single most important property of a harness is that it does not marry you to one model vendor or one hosting arrangement. Models improve on a cadence of months, pricing shifts underneath running workloads, and your compliance requirements may force some inference on-premises or into a specific cloud region. A harness that speaks only to its maker's hosted models converts every one of those changes into a migration.

Concretely, ask three questions of any candidate. Can it use models from more than one provider? Can it point at self-hosted or private-endpoint inference, and does that path get real support rather than a community workaround? Are its configurations, prompts, and tool definitions exportable in a form the next harness could import? A yes to all three means a model deprecation is a configuration change. A no means it is a project.

## Criterion two: interface fit to the role

The whitepaper's second selection rule, a user-friendly interface, sounds soft until you price its absence. A harness only produces value through the people using it, and workspace configuration depends on the individual's role. Forcing a terminal on your finance team guarantees quiet non-adoption, and the shadow-AI usage that replaces it will run outside every control you built. Forcing a heavyweight graphical environment on engineers who work in terminals produces the same evasion in the other direction.

Expect to run more than one harness, on purpose. What must stay uniform is everything behind the interface, including identity, permissioning, and audit. Evaluate each candidate against the least technical person who will use it daily, and involve those people in the pilot rather than surveying them after the decision.

## Criterion three: permissioning, audit, and revocability

The harness is where an agent's toolbelt gets loaded, which makes it the first enforcement point for least privilege. A role-specific toolbelt means the finance analyst's agent carries finance tools and credentials and cannot reach the deployment pipeline, and that restriction is configured centrally per role rather than assembled by hand on each laptop.

Three capabilities matter here. The harness must support centrally managed, role-scoped tool and credential configuration. It must log agent actions in a form your audit systems can consume, tied to the named human operator behind the session. And access must be revocable immediately when behavior is flagged, without waiting for a laptop to sync. Most current harnesses expose lifecycle hooks that let policy checks run before and after every tool invocation, which is the mechanism that turns these requirements from vendor promises into your own enforced code.

## Criterion four: open protocol support

Tool integrations are the largest sunk cost a harness accumulates, and the way to keep that investment portable is to build it on an open standard rather than a vendor's plugin format. The **Model Context Protocol** has become that standard. Anthropic [donated MCP to the Agentic AI Foundation under the Linux Foundation](https://www.anthropic.com/news/donating-the-model-context-protocol-and-establishing-of-the-agentic-ai-foundation) in December 2025, with OpenAI, Google, Microsoft, and AWS among the backers, and it is now supported natively across every major harness and agent platform.

The practical consequence is that an MCP server you build for one harness works in the next one. Connections to your knowledge graph, ticketing system, and internal APIs survive a harness migration intact, which shrinks the switching cost that criterion one is designed to protect. Treat native MCP support as a hard requirement, and treat any harness that pushes a proprietary integration format as a bet against your own exit options.

## Running the decision

Weight the criteria by what they cost when they fail. Flexibility failures cost a migration. Interface failures cost adoption. Permissioning failures cost an incident, and protocol failures cost your integration investment. In my experience the interface criterion is the one enterprises undervalue, because the people running the evaluation are rarely the people who will use the tool.

The selection process itself can be small. Pick two candidates from different points on the spectrum, put each in front of five real users from different roles for two weeks, and score them against the four criteria with the failure costs in mind. Before any of that, confirm the foundations the harness will stand on, because a harness pointed at ungoverned data produces well-interfaced wrong answers. The [readiness checklist](/notebook/the-ai-readiness-checklist-annotated) is where that confirmation starts, and the harness pilot is what it should feed into.
