Most AI readiness problems are discovered after the budget is committed, which is the most expensive possible time to discover them. Gartner [predicts](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-02-26-lack-of-ai-ready-data-puts-ai-projects-at-risk) that through 2026, organizations will abandon 60% of AI projects that are not supported by AI-ready data, and every item on that abandonment invoice was visible before the project started.

The whitepaper I wrote, [Intelligent Business: A Modular Approach to AI Integration](/papers/intelligent-business), closes with a Pre-Implementation Checklist of eleven items. The checklist is deliberately terse, because a checklist should be. This article is the annotation: for each item, what it actually means, what failing looks like in practice, and the one concrete action that starts the work. Treat it as the agenda for your readiness assessment. Several of the failure symptoms below will be familiar if you have read [Why AI Projects Fail](/notebook/why-ai-projects-fail); this is the working document for fixing them in advance.

## 1. Data assessment

**What it means.** Evaluate how your data is classified, maintained, and ultimately understood. This is the umbrella item the other ten hang from. An AI system is only as capable as its underlying data and integrations, so before anything else you need an honest picture of what data you have, where it lives, who is responsible for it, and how much of it can be trusted.

**What failing looks like.** Nobody in the organization can produce a current inventory of data sources. The ERP holds the financial truth, the CRM holds a different version of the customer, a dozen departmental spreadsheets hold the corrections everyone actually uses, and the AI project starts by pointing an agent at all of them simultaneously. The output failures that follow get labeled as model problems.

**First action.** Inventory the core business platforms, including the ERP, CRM, HR, and accounting systems, plus the derived and synthetic datasets built on top of them. For each one, record the owner, the access method, and the last time anyone verified its accuracy. The gaps in that table are your readiness backlog.

## 2. Ontology alignment

**What it means.** Convene leadership, engineering, and product stakeholders to establish a unified **ontological framework**, which is a shared semantic language for the business. The nouns and verbs of your company, meaning terms such as customer, active account, and order complete, get agreed on once and enforced everywhere.

**What failing looks like.** Each department holds a private definition of the same core terms, and the definitions contradict each other in ways nobody has written down. An AI system inherits every definition and every contradiction. When it answers a revenue question using the finance definition of a customer and a churn question using the marketing definition, the inconsistency reads as hallucination, and trust in the system erodes before the real problem is even diagnosed.

**First action.** Schedule the alignment meeting with named attendees from leadership, engineering, and product. The meeting has one deliverable, which is a first-draft shared vocabulary covering the ten or so terms your pilot workflow depends on. Deep company insight is required, which is why McKinsey [attributes a 3.8x performance improvement](https://www.mckinsey.com/featured-insights/week-in-charts/exec-endorsement-fuels-ai-adoption) to AI initiatives with engaged executive leadership. This meeting is where that engagement starts.

## 3. Data stewardship

**What it means.** Appoint dedicated **data product owners** responsible for the integrity of core business data systems. Your data now has users, and some of them are automated, so it needs what any product with users needs, namely a named owner, quality standards, and a roadmap.

**What failing looks like.** Data quality issues have no owner, so they have no fix. A discrepancy is noticed, a ticket is filed, the ticket bounces between IT and the business unit for a month, and the agent keeps consuming the flawed records the entire time. Ownership disputes that were tolerable when humans read the reports become operational incidents when agents act on them.

**First action.** Name an owner for each of the three or four systems your pilot will touch. Business units should own their domain data, because subject matter experts recognize the key data points fastest and a centralized IT bottleneck will not scale. Write the names down and publish them, because ownership that is not visible to the rest of the organization will not be exercised.

## 4. Governance planning

**What it means.** Develop a formal change management policy for evolving the ontological framework as the business grows. The shared vocabulary from item two is a living artifact. Products launch, org charts change, acquisitions bring entire competing vocabularies in the door, and the framework has to absorb all of it without fracturing.

**What failing looks like.** The alignment meeting happens once, the vocabulary is documented with real care, and then it is never touched again. Eighteen months later the business has reorganized, half the terms have drifted, and the ontology describes a company that no longer exists. Agents grounded in it are now confidently wrong, and the organization concludes that the alignment work was wasted rather than unmaintained.

**First action.** Write the change process before the first change arrives. It fits on a page: who may propose a change to a shared definition, who approves it, and how an approved change propagates to the systems that depend on it. Assign the maintenance of that page to a named role rather than a person, so it survives turnover.

## 5. Data privacy and PII obfuscation

**What it means.** Implement automated pipelines that detect and mask personally identifiable information and sensitive financial data before it enters the AI orchestration layers. The word that matters is before. Masking applied at the source keeps every downstream layer, including caches, logs, and prompts, out of scope for the most painful compliance conversations.

**What failing looks like.** PII flows into prompts, prompts flow into logs, and logs flow into whatever retention regime the logging vendor defaults to. Nobody decided to expose customer data; the pipeline simply never had a masking stage, and now the audit has to treat every component that ever saw a prompt as a system of record for personal data. Remediation at that point means re-architecting under regulatory pressure.

**First action.** Put automated detection and masking at the ingestion boundary, where data enters the pipeline, and verify it with synthetic records that look like your real ones. A test dataset of fake customers with realistic names, account numbers, and card patterns will tell you quickly whether the masking actually catches what your business handles.

## 6. Data retention and lifecycle management

**What it means.** Establish strict archiving rules so AI models query current, relevant data rather than drawing conclusions from outdated or deprecated legacy information. Every dataset has a shelf life, and an agent has no instinct for staleness. A human analyst hesitates when a price list looks old. An agent cites it with full confidence.

**What failing looks like.** The agent answers a customer question using a price list that was superseded two quarters ago, or routes an approval to a manager who left during the last reorganization. Each individual error is small and explainable. The pattern is corrosive, because users learn they must verify everything the system says, at which point the system is producing work rather than saving it.

**First action.** For each dataset your pilot touches, define what expired means in business terms, whether that is a date, a superseding version, or a status flag, and archive on that rule automatically. Archived data should remain available for audit and history, and it should be unavailable to agents answering questions about the present.

## 7. API and integration standardization

**What it means.** Audit existing data silos to ensure they can be accessed through modern, well-documented APIs, preventing custom-coded, brittle connections that break during system updates. Programmatic access to your core and derived data is a non-negotiable requirement, and how that access is built determines whether the integration survives contact with a vendor upgrade.

**What failing looks like.** The connection to a core system is a screen scraper, a nightly CSV export, or an undocumented database query someone wrote in 2019. It works until the vendor ships an update, and then it fails silently. The agent keeps operating on the last data it received, and the failure surfaces weeks later as a pattern of subtly wrong answers rather than a visible outage anyone would investigate.

**First action.** For every source system in your inventory, record how data actually gets out of it today. Flag anything that is not a documented, versioned API. That flagged list, ordered by how critical the system is to the pilot, is your integration backlog, and it is better discovered now than during the incident review.

## 8. Unstructured data triage

**What it means.** Define a clear strategy for processing and classifying unstructured enterprise data, meaning the contracts, emails, meeting notes, and slide decks where much of the institutional knowledge lives, so the semantic layer can reliably ingest and understand it.

**What failing looks like.** The project connects the entire corporate file share to a vector store on the theory that more context is better. Retrieval quality collapses, because the store now contains six drafts of every document alongside the final version, with no way to tell them apart. Worse, documents that were access-restricted in the file system are now findable by anyone who phrases a question the right way. The shortcut produces a data governance incident rather than a knowledge base.

**First action.** Resist ingesting everything. Pick the two or three document types your pilot workflow genuinely needs, define how each is processed, classified, and permissioned, and ingest only those. Expand deliberately from a working foundation.

## 9. Data freshness SLAs

**What it means.** Establish service level agreements for critical datasets so autonomous agents make decisions on real-time or near-real-time information. A freshness SLA states how old a given dataset is allowed to be and what happens when it exceeds that age.

**What failing looks like.** Nobody has measured staleness, so nobody knows the inventory feed the agent relies on updates nightly while the workflow assumes it is current. The agent promises stock that sold out at ten this morning. When the failure is investigated, the answer to how fresh is this data turns out to be a shrug, which means the same failure will recur on a different dataset next month.

**First action.** For each pilot dataset, document two numbers, namely how often it actually updates today and how fresh the workflow needs it to be. Where the second number is smaller than the first, you have found a gap to close or a workflow to redesign before an agent depends on it.

## 10. Cost allocation and compute chargeback

**What it means.** Develop a tracking system that maps AI data queries and model inferences back to specific departments, preventing runaway costs stemming from inefficient data architecture. Inference is a metered operational expense, and metering only works if every request carries an identifier saying who it ran for and which workflow it served.

**What failing looks like.** Every team shares one API key, and finance receives one aggregate monthly bill that nobody can decompose. There is no way to say what a given workflow costs per completion, which means there is no way to say whether it pays for itself, and an initiative that cannot state its unit economics cannot defend its budget when the spending review arrives.

**First action.** Tag every request with a department and workflow identifier from the first day of the pilot. Retrofitting attribution onto months of untagged usage is somewhere between painful and impossible, while carrying the tags from day one is nearly free.

## 11. Data incident response plan

**What it means.** Create a standard operating procedure for quickly identifying, isolating, and correcting data poisoning or logical errors when an agent makes a decision based on flawed information. Data incidents differ from outages. Nothing goes down. The system keeps running and produces confident, wrong output, and your existing incident runbooks do not cover it.

**What failing looks like.** A flawed record is discovered in a source system, and the immediate question is what the agents did with it during the weeks it was wrong. Without a plan and without a replayable record of agent activity, the honest answer is that nobody knows, and the remediation becomes a manual audit of everything the system touched.

**First action.** Write the runbook now, structured as identify, isolate, correct, and replay. The replay step is what makes the other three tractable, and it depends on architecture you must choose deliberately. I cover the full procedure in [What Data Poisoning Means for Your Business](/notebook/what-data-poisoning-means-for-your-business).

## Working the list

Read as a whole, the checklist has a shape. Items one through four establish what your data means and who answers for it. Five through nine make the data safe, current, and reachable. Ten and eleven prepare you for the two certainties of operation, which are that the system will cost money and that something will eventually go wrong. None of the eleven requires purchasing anything, and most of them start with a conversation and a document.

That is the practical point. Work through the checklist honestly and you will know your readiness before a vendor contract is signed, while the fixes are still cheap. The first step is a meeting, not a purchase order. Put the eleven items on its agenda.
