Somewhere in your organization, an AI agent is probably operating with more access than a new employee would receive on their first day. Broad access is easy to configure, the pilot has a deadline, and scoping permissions properly feels like work that can wait. Gartner [predicts that more than 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by the end of 2027](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-06-25-gartner-predicts-over-40-percent-of-agentic-ai-projects-will-be-canceled-by-end-of-2027), and alongside escalating costs and unclear business value, the cause it names is inadequate risk controls.

This article is about the control layer that prevents that outcome. It is the second entry in The Architecture, One Piece at a Time, a series walking through the design from my whitepaper; the [series introduction](/notebook/queue-workers-knowledge-graph) covers the queue, the workers, and the knowledge graph that every later entry builds on. This entry covers Layer 2 of the six-layer model, the **Trust & Policy Boundary**, and the discipline it enforces.

## The machine identity problem arrived before the agents did

The identity systems in most enterprises were designed for people, and the population they now govern is mostly machines. CyberArk's [2025 Identity Security Landscape](https://www.cyberark.com/press/machine-identities-outnumber-humans-by-more-than-80-to-1-new-report-exposes-the-exponential-threats-of-fragmented-identity-security/) counted 82 machine identities for every human identity in the average organization, found that 42% of those machine identities hold privileged or sensitive access, and found that 88% of organizations still define a "privileged user" as a human. Service accounts, API keys, and workload identities have been accumulating for a decade, largely unknown and largely uncontrolled, and the governance never caught up.

Agents change the character of that exposure. A leaked service account key is a passive liability until someone finds it. An agent is an active one, because it pursues goals, chains tools together, and operates at machine speed, so a permission mistake propagates faster than a human review cycle can catch it. The gap is already measurable: IBM's [Cost of a Data Breach Report 2025](https://www.ibm.com/downloads/documents/us-en/131cf87b20b31c91) found that 97% of organizations that suffered an AI-related security incident lacked proper AI access controls.

**Zero trust** is the discipline that closes the gap. The term has collected marketing baggage over the years, so it is worth stating plainly what it means in this architecture: no actor is trusted because of where it sits on the network, and no actor is trusted because it authenticated once earlier in the session. Identity and permissions are verified at every step, for every actor, human or machine.

## Verification at every step

The physical equivalent is how a well-run secure facility handles a visiting contractor. The contractor is signed in by a named employee who takes responsibility for the visit. They are issued a badge that opens only the doors their job requires, and the badge is checked at every one of those doors rather than only at the lobby. At the end of the day the badge stops working, whether or not anyone remembers to collect it. Nothing about the arrangement implies the contractor is dishonest; the facility simply does not run on assumptions about character.

Layer 2 applies that procedure to every AI actor in the system. The boundary sits between the agentic workstations where requests originate and everything the business actually depends on. Every critical request passes through gatekeepers that verify the actor's identity and credentials, validate the specific action being requested, and confirm the human authorization behind it. An agent that passed those checks five minutes ago passes them again on its next request, because the previous approval attached to the previous task and expired with it.

The remaining sections walk through the four commitments that make the boundary real: permissions scoped to a single task, credentials that expire on their own, a named human behind every action, and a write path the model can never touch directly.

## Permissions scoped to a single task

Traditional access control assigns permissions to roles, and roles accumulate. An employee who changes teams three times in five years usually retains fragments of access from each stop, because revocation depends on someone noticing. Applied to agents, role-based thinking produces the standing service account with database write access that no one can fully explain two quarters later.

The architecture scopes permissions to tasks instead. An agent dispatched to reconcile invoices receives read access to the invoice dataset and write access to one staging area, for the duration of that task, and nothing else. When the task completes, the grant is gone. Privilege creep becomes structurally impossible, because there is no long-lived entitlement for privileges to creep into: the permission set is constructed when the task is dispatched and discarded when it completes.

Task scoping also caps the blast radius of the failure modes agents actually exhibit. A model that misinterprets an instruction, or is manipulated through a poisoned input, can only act within the grant it currently holds. The security review stops needing to reason about everything the agent could conceivably do and starts reasoning about what this task authorizes, which is a question small enough to answer well.

This is stricter than most human access policies, and it should be. An agent does not need to check email or open a spreadsheet between tasks. It exists to execute discrete units of work, which means its access can be exactly as discrete.

## Credentials that expire on their own

All credentials issued to AI actors in this design are temporary and expire automatically. The distinction between revocation and expiry matters more than it first appears. Revocation is an action someone must take, which means it depends on detection, attention, and follow-through. Expiry is a default the system enforces on its own. A forgotten agent credential in this architecture is not a lingering vulnerability, because forgetting is the case the design already handled.

Humans in the system reauthenticate at regular intervals, and identity is correlated to access through secure tokens, so a presented credential always resolves to a specific verified actor. When behavior is flagged as abnormal, access is instantly revocable for human and AI actors alike; a later entry in this series, [Secure and Reliable Access](/notebook/secure-and-reliable-access), covers the revocation machinery in detail.

## Every action ties to a named human

The requirement that does the most organizational work is also the simplest to state: every automated action is directly tied to the named human operator who requested or authorized it, through a verifiable authorization chain.

This is what turns an audit question from a crisis into a lookup. When security asks who authorized an agent's write to the contract system last Tuesday, the answer is a name, retrieved from a record, in seconds. It is also what keeps accountability where it belongs. Software cannot be accountable; it can only be traceable, and traceability back to a person is what makes delegation to an agent defensible in the first place. I have sat in the reviews where this question gets asked, and the projects that can answer it with a name survive the meeting.

The named-operator rule also disciplines the humans. An operator whose name attaches to every action an agent takes on their behalf thinks differently about what they dispatch, in the same way an engineer whose name is on the change record reviews the change more carefully.

## The model never writes directly to critical systems

The final commitment is the one that most directly addresses what language models actually are. LLM output is probabilistic, which makes it well suited to proposing, drafting, and analyzing, and poorly suited to being the last step before a change lands in a system of record. So in this architecture it never is. Language models never write directly to critical data sources. A proposed change is expressed as a structured request, tested in an isolated environment with verifiable results, confirmed against the operator's authorization, and then executed by a tested, audited write path whose behavior is deterministic.

The agent still gets its work done, and the write still happens at machine speed once cleared. What changes is that the nondeterministic component is confined to the part of the workflow where nondeterminism is useful. Every action that mutates core data passes through the same policy boundary, which means the audit trail exists because the system cannot operate without producing one. That property, compliance emerging from normal operation, is the subject of a later series entry, [Compliance as a Byproduct](/notebook/compliance-as-a-byproduct).

## What the boundary buys you

In [Why AI Projects Fail](/notebook/why-ai-projects-fail), the missing trust boundary appears as failure mode four: security and compliance ask who authorized an action, what the system can touch, and whether access can be revoked right now, and the project stalls because nobody can answer. Layer 2 exists so that every one of those questions has a fast, factual answer. That transforms the security team's relationship to the initiative. They become the reviewers who approved the design early, because the design finally gives them something worth approving, and a rollout carrying their signature does not get halted by their questions.

These controls are also cheaper early than late. Retrofitting task-scoped identity onto a pilot that shipped with a shared service account means rebuilding the pilot; building on the boundary from the first day means the demo and the production deployment are the same system.

## Where to start

The first artifact is an inventory. List every credential your AI systems currently hold, including the experiments and the shadow deployments, and for each one record three facts: whether it is standing or temporary, what exactly it can touch, and which named person is responsible for its actions. Expect the list to be uncomfortable. Then take the worst entry, the standing credential with the broadest scope and the least clear owner, and make it the first one you convert to task-scoped, auto-expiring access under a named operator.

The full six-layer design that Layer 2 protects is laid out in [Intelligent Business: A Modular Approach to AI Integration](/papers/intelligent-business). The credential inventory does not require a single line of it to be built, which is exactly why it is the right first step. Run it this week.
