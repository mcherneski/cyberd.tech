An AI system is only as good as the ways people and software can reach it, and access is where most enterprise integrations quietly rot. The connective tissue is usually built ad hoc, one custom connection at a time, by whoever needed it first, and MuleSoft's [2025 Connectivity Benchmark](https://www.salesforce.com/news/stories/connectivity-report-announcement-2025/), surveying 1,050 enterprise IT leaders, measured the result: the average enterprise runs 897 applications, and only 29% of them are integrated.

Layering agents onto that landscape without a deliberate access design produces a predictable shape. Every team wires its own path to the model, every agent reaches business systems through a different homemade connector, and nobody can enumerate the paths, let alone shut one down quickly. This article is the fifth entry in The Architecture, One Piece at a Time, the series walking through the design from my whitepaper (the [series introduction](/notebook/queue-workers-knowledge-graph) covers the core machinery). It covers the access surface: how humans and agents actually touch the system, and how that touch stays governable.

## The agentic harness

The most common AI interface in this architecture is the **agentic harness**. A harness equips an agent with a preconfigured, role-specific toolbelt: the tools it may invoke, the data connections it may use, the model or models behind it, and the permission profile it carries, all assembled before the agent performs any work.

The comparison that earns its keep here is the difference between a tradesperson's belt and a hardware store. An electrician arrives at a job carrying the specific tools the job requires, selected in advance by someone who understood the work. Nobody hands the electrician the keys to the whole store and trusts the job description to constrain what gets picked up. An unharnessed agent, prompted into existence with broad credentials and told to be helpful, is the hardware store scenario, and it is how most ungoverned pilots operate today. The harness is the belt: capability is decided at configuration time, by a human who owns the role definition, rather than at run time by the model's judgment about what it needs.

Harness selection is a longevity decision, because the harness is the layer your organization will touch daily long after this year's model is retired. Two criteria matter most. The harness should allow model and inference hosting flexibility, so you can swap providers or move inference on-premise without rebuilding every role definition, and it should offer an interface your actual workforce will use, because a technically superior harness that only engineers tolerate becomes a shadow-IT generator. Permissioning depth belongs on the list as well: a harness that cannot express task-scoped grants and named-operator identity will fight the trust boundary instead of feeding it. All three criteria are applications of the framework's modularity rule to the one component with the most daily human contact.

## Workstations sized to the role

Around the harness sits the human side of Layer 1, the **agentic workstation**: the digital workspace where a person originates requests and reviews results. The design principle is that workstation configuration follows role, and the range is wide by intent. A developer may need only a terminal. An analyst may work through a chat surface bound to governed data connections. An operations manager may need a full workstation application with dashboards, approval queues, and manual overrides.

Resisting the single-standard-interface instinct matters more than it first appears. Forcing every role through one interface guarantees the interface fits almost nobody, and people who are poorly fit by the sanctioned tool go find an unsanctioned one. Adoption follows fit, and every user the sanctioned surface loses is a user the governance loses with them. The governance stays uniform underneath the variety: every workstation, from the barest terminal to the richest application, authenticates the same way, carries a named operator identity, tracks its token allocation, and reaches business systems only through the same policy boundary described in the [zero trust entry](/notebook/zero-trust-for-ai-agents). The interface flexes while the identity and audit requirements stay constant.

## APIs are the contract, not the plumbing

Beneath harnesses and workstations sits the question of how any of this reaches your actual business systems, and this is where the whitepaper's Pre-Implementation Checklist is blunt: audit existing data silos to ensure they can be accessed through modern, well-documented APIs, because the alternative is custom-coded, brittle connections that break during system updates.

The brittleness compounds in an agentic system. A human using a fragile integration notices when it breaks and works around it. A worker claiming tasks from a queue does not improvise; it fails, retries, and fills a dead-letter queue, and if the connector was custom-built by a developer who has since left, the failure arrives with no documentation and no owner. I have inherited enough of these connectors in enterprise environments to state the pattern with confidence: the integration built in a sprint costs its build price again every year, paid in outages and in the fear of upgrading the system on either end of it. Multiply that by an estate where 71% of applications have no integration at all, and API standardization reveals itself as the precondition for reliable automation.

Standardization means each business system exposes one documented, versioned interface that every consumer uses, human tools and agent harnesses alike. One interface per system means one place to enforce authentication, one place to apply rate limits, one place to watch for abnormal usage, and one contract to maintain through upgrades. It is also what makes the modular architecture practical: components can only be swapped independently if the seams between them are standard.

## Revocation in seconds

The final property of the access layer is the one your security team will ask about first. The framework's Secure Access principle requires that access be instantly revocable for human and AI actors the moment behavior is flagged as abnormal.

Instant revocation is an architectural property, and it has to be designed in rather than added. It falls out of decisions made elsewhere in this series. Because every actor authenticates against central identity infrastructure rather than holding scattered local accounts, disabling one identity severs every session and pathway it holds. Because AI credentials are temporary and auto-expiring, there is no drawer of forgotten long-lived keys that revocation might miss. Because all access flows through standardized APIs and the policy boundary, there is no side door that stays open after the front door is shut. An organization with 897 applications and hand-built connectors cannot honestly answer the question "can you shut this agent out right now?" The architecture in this series exists so the answer is yes, in seconds, with evidence.

Revocation also needs to be cheap to invoke, organizationally, and this is where otherwise sound designs fail in practice. If cutting off a suspicious agent requires a change ticket and a meeting, operators will hesitate, and hesitation at machine speed is expensive. The correct posture is the one fire alarms embody: pulling it is encouraged whenever the evidence warrants, the disruption is bounded and recoverable, and a false alarm costs far less than a real fire ignored. A revoked agent's unfinished tasks are not lost; they remain on the queue, intact and auditable, ready to be reassigned once the flag is resolved.

## What this layer buys the whole design

Access is where the architecture meets its users, and it is the layer most tempting to improvise because improvised access works fine at demo scale. The cost arrives later, as the connector nobody owns, the interface nobody uses, and the agent nobody can shut off, and those are precisely the foundational gaps that [Why AI Projects Fail](/notebook/why-ai-projects-fail) traces most enterprise AI losses back to. Deliberate access design, meaning harnesses configured per role, workstations sized to the person, one standard interface per system, and revocation that takes seconds, is what makes the rest of the stack usable by an actual workforce and defensible in front of an actual security review.

The full design, including how the access layer feeds the audit store covered in the next entry, is in [Intelligent Business: A Modular Approach to AI Integration](/papers/intelligent-business). The practical first step is an integration audit scoped to one week: list the systems your AI efforts actually need to reach, and for each one record whether it has a documented API, who owns that interface, and how you would revoke an agent's access to it today. The systems that fail all three questions are your roadmap, in priority order.
