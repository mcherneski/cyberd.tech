When an AI system fails, your organization's instincts and runbooks assume something goes down. A data incident inverts that assumption. The system stays up, responds quickly, and produces confident output that happens to be wrong, because somewhere upstream an agent consumed data that was flawed, stale, or deliberately corrupted.

The deliberate case has a name, **data poisoning**, and it is more practical for an attacker than most executives assume. The accidental case is more common and has no special name; a botched migration, a broken feed, or a wrong entry in a source system produces the same result once agents faithfully propagate it. Both cases fail the same way and both are handled by the same procedure, which is why the [Intelligent Business](/papers/intelligent-business) Pre-Implementation Checklist closes with a data incident response plan. This article explains what that plan defends against and what it looks like.

## The threat is cheaper than you think

The instinct that poisoning requires corrupting some meaningful percentage of a training corpus turns out to be wrong. A joint study by Anthropic, the UK AI Security Institute, and the Alan Turing Institute [found that as few as 250 malicious documents](https://www.anthropic.com/research/small-samples-poison) can backdoor a large language model regardless of its size, even when the largest models tested trained on more than twenty times the clean data. The attacker's cost does not scale with the target. Producing a few hundred documents is trivial.

For most enterprises, though, the more immediate exposure is not the model's training data at all. It is the business data your agents retrieve at runtime. An agent grounded in your inventory system, your CRM, and your document store trusts whatever those sources say. A poisoned record, a vendor feed altered upstream, or a plausible-looking document planted in a shared drive enters the agent's reasoning with the same authority as your cleanest audited table. The agent then acts, meaning it answers customers, drafts approvals, and updates records, and each action launders the flawed input into legitimate-looking output that other people and other systems rely on in turn.

That laundering effect is what makes data incidents a governance problem rather than a purely technical one. By the time a human notices something wrong, the wrongness has a paper trail.

## The structural defense

You cannot review your way out of this exposure one output at a time, and models will not save you by becoming more skeptical. The defense has to be structural, and the whitepaper states it as a core principle: AI-generated and retrieved data is referenced against trusted **systems of record** before it drives an action.

The principle sounds abstract until you place it in a workflow. An agent drafts a refund. Before the refund executes, the order details in the draft are checked against the order management system, the governed source of truth for orders, rather than against whatever documents or cached context the agent reasoned from. If the draft claims facts the system of record does not support, the action stops and a human looks at it. The agent's reasoning can be poisoned; the verification path cannot, because it never consults the material the agent was fed.

Two consequences follow. First, verification is only as good as the systems of record behind it, which is why the unglamorous work of stewardship, freshness SLAs, and retention rules in the [readiness checklist](/notebook/the-ai-readiness-checklist-annotated) functions as a security control. Second, language models never write directly to critical systems; changes pass through tested, audited paths where these checks live. Verification limits how much damage flawed data can do. When some damage gets through anyway, you need the procedure.

## The procedure: identify, isolate, correct, replay

A data incident response plan is a standard operating procedure your organization writes and rehearses before the incident, with four stages.

**Identify.** Determine what data is flawed and when it went bad. The trigger is usually a human noticing an output that fails the smell test, which is one more reason to keep skilled people reviewing AI-assisted work. The identification question is bounded and specific: which records, which source, wrong since when.

**Isolate.** Stop the spread before starting the cleanup. Quarantine the affected source so agents no longer read from it, suspend the workflows that depend on it, and revoke or restrict the credentials involved if the corruption looks deliberate. This stage is where instant revocability, one of the whitepaper's core principles, stops being a compliance checkbox and starts being the tool you reach for.

**Correct.** Fix the source data, restore from a known-good state, or mark the affected records untrusted until a steward rules on them. Correction belongs to the data product owner named for that system, which is one more argument for having appointed one before the incident rather than during it.

**Replay.** Answer the question that determines the blast radius: what did agents do with the flawed data while it was live? This is the stage most organizations discover they cannot perform.

## Why replay requires the architecture

Replay is only possible if the record of agent activity still exists in a usable form. In the architecture the whitepaper describes, every task an agent or automated worker performs travels through a central event queue as a message carrying the authorization, the instructions, and the data for that task, and messages are never destroyed. Think of the queue as a mail room that photocopies every envelope it sorts. The work is delivered and completed, and the copies remain in a read-only archive with the durable audit store.

During an incident, that archive is your forensic record. Investigators filter the message history for tasks that touched the poisoned source during the affected window and reconstruct exactly which actions consumed the bad data, which outputs it shaped, and which downstream systems and customers were affected. Remediation stops being an estimate. Where a task read flawed input, it can be re-run against the corrected data and its output compared with the original, which turns the blast radius from a guess into a list.

Organizations that skipped this foundation face the alternative, which is a manual audit of weeks of agent activity across whatever partial logs each component happened to keep. The difference between the two experiences is decided years before the incident, in the architecture selection. It is one of the concrete payoffs of the event-driven design that rarely makes the sales pitch, and one of the quieter reasons projects without such foundations end up in the failure statistics covered in [Why AI Projects Fail](/notebook/why-ai-projects-fail).

## Write the plan this week

The practical output of this article is a document of a few pages. Name the incident commander role and the data product owners who own correction. Define the identification triggers, the isolation steps per critical source, and where the replayable record lives, or note honestly that it does not exist yet, which is itself a finding that should shape your architecture roadmap. Then schedule a tabletop exercise with one scenario, such as a corrupted vendor feed discovered ten days late, and walk the four stages with the people who would actually perform them.

Your organization already runs this drill for security breaches and for outages. Flawed data driving automated decisions is now a peer of those risks, and it deserves the same rehearsed, boring, written-down response.
