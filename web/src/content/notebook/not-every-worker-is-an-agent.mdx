Enterprises are drafting elaborate governance regimes for AI agents while their existing automations run with far less oversight. The scheduled job that has synchronized customer records every night since 2019 holds standing credentials nobody has reviewed, and the new agent that drafts meeting summaries is about to receive its own risk committee.

The split feels reasonable because the agent is new and the batch job is familiar, but it does not survive contact with how these systems actually operate. In the architecture I describe in my whitepaper, a serverless function that reformats documents and a frontier-model agent that performs multi-step analysis claim tasks from the same queue, and they carry identical identity, permission, and audit requirements. Once you see why, most of what is currently being sold as AI governance resolves into a discipline your organization already understands.

## Two workers, one queue

The design centers on a central event queue. Think of it as a secure message sorting line, with tasks represented as different colored envelopes. Workers watch the line for their assigned colors, open the envelope, attempt the task inside, and report the result back. Envelopes are never destroyed, even when a task fails or a message arrives malformed. They can be retried, analyzed, and produced later as audit evidence, and each one carries the routing labels, the instructions, the data, and the authorization needed to complete the work.

The worker at the end of that line varies enormously. One is a serverless function that finishes its task in under a second. Another is a scheduled batch job that runs for an hour. A third is an agent reasoning with a frontier model across a dozen tool calls. From the queue's perspective, these workers are indistinguishable. Each one presents a verified identity, claims only the messages it is permitted to claim, executes with only the permissions that specific task requires, and leaves a permanent record of what it did. Each action traces back to the named human operator on whose authority the work happens.

Nothing in that description mentions intelligence. Identity, least privilege, traceable authorization, and a durable record are required whether the worker is forty lines of Python or a model with a million-token context window. The whitepaper compresses this into a single line about the orchestration layer: one audit spine for every automation, intelligent or not.

## The questions are older than the agent

When a security team evaluates an AI agent, it asks a familiar set of questions. Who authorized this system? What exactly can it touch? Can its access be revoked right now? What did it do last week? These are the right questions, and they are the same questions most enterprises cannot answer about the automation they already run.

Walk through a typical environment and you will find service accounts with broad standing permissions, scripts with credentials pasted into configuration files, and integrations built by contractors who left years ago. These systems persist because they work, and because nobody wants to be the person who breaks the nightly sync. They enjoy a quiet trust that was never earned through controls; it accumulated through uneventful operation.

AI agents are refused that quiet trust, and the refusal is correct. An agent's behavior is variable enough that no serious security team will extend it the benefit of the doubt. The useful move is to notice that the skepticism was always warranted for the whole fleet. The agent did not create the governance gap in your automation estate; it made the gap impossible to keep ignoring.

## What a single spine buys

Holding every worker to the same contract has consequences beyond tidiness. There is one trust and policy boundary where identity is verified and actions are validated before anything mutates core data, rather than a per-system patchwork. There is one durable audit store where every action lands, which means compliance evidence accumulates as a byproduct of normal operation instead of being assembled by hand before each audit. A missing trust boundary is one of the five failure modes in [Why AI Projects Fail](/notebook/why-ai-projects-fail), and it stalls scripted automation projects just as reliably as agentic ones.

The uniform contract also makes workers swappable, which matters more than it first appears. Suppose a pilot reveals that a task assigned to a frontier-model agent is actually deterministic, and a plain function can do it at a fraction of the cost. Because both workers meet the same identity and audit requirements, you replace one with the other without touching the governance model. The reverse swap works the same way when a scripted job turns out to need judgment. The decision about which worker handles a task becomes an economic and quality decision, made freely, because the control surface never changes.

There is an organizational benefit as well, and in my experience it is the one that moves executives. A proposal for "AI governance" sounds like a new discipline requiring a new committee, a new vendor category, and a new budget line. A proposal for automation governance describes something the organization already funds: access reviews, change management, service account hygiene. Extending those controls uniformly across every worker, human-triggered or scheduled or agentic, is an expansion of existing practice. It asks the security team to enforce one standard everywhere rather than to invent a second one.

## Where to begin

The practical first step is an inventory. List every automation that touches business data, regardless of how it is triggered or how intelligent it is. For each entry, record four things: the identity it runs under, the permissions it holds compared with the permissions it needs, the named person accountable for it, and where the record of its actions lives. The list will be longer than you expect, and the answers will be thinner than you would like.

That inventory becomes the foundation for everything that follows. Systems that cannot answer the four questions get migrated onto the spine or retired, and every new worker, agent or otherwise, connects through the queue and inherits the full control set on day one. Run the exercise honestly and the debate about special rules for AI mostly dissolves, because the category that needs governing is automation, not intelligence.
