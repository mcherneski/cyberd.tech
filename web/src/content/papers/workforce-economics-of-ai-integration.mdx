## Executive Summary

- The case against AI-driven workforce reduction is settled enough to state briefly: companies spend approximately [$1.27 for every dollar saved](https://www.forbes.com/councils/forbestechcouncil/2026/04/24/why-companies-regret-laying-off-workers-for-ai/) through AI-driven cuts, [55% of employers who restructured for AI regret it](https://hrexecutive.com/the-ai-layoff-trap-why-half-will-be-quietly-rehired/), and Gartner projects that [half of the companies that cut customer service staff citing AI will rehire for similar functions by 2027](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2026-02-03-gartner-predicts-half-of-companies-that-cut-customer-service-staff-due-to-ai-will-rehire-by-2027). This paper takes that evidence as its starting point, not its subject.
- The subject is execution. Retraining and redeployment succeed or fail on two operational questions: where the opportunities are, and how the program is run. Both have documented answers.
- Opportunities are found at the task level rather than the role level. The methods covered here are the task inventory, the automation-augmentation task profiles, skills adjacency analysis, internal skills supply mapping, and the deliberate examination of what the automation could not do, which is how IKEA's Ingka Group found a €1.3 billion sales channel inside its call centers.
- Program design determines returns. In a BCG and Harvard Business School survey of 1,215 US companies, only [40% of reskilling programs returned their investment](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/five-ways-to-make-the-most-of-reskilling-investments), but programs that piloted for more than a year before scaling succeeded at 61% versus 26% without a pilot, and opt-in programs were roughly 1.5 times more likely to pay than mandated ones.
- The recurring failure is treating reskilling as a courseware purchase owned by the learning department. The evidence from BCG's Digital Reskilling Lab and McKinsey's skill-transformation research points the same direction: reskilling is a change-management exercise spanning demand analysis, supply analysis, manager involvement, on-the-job learning, and job matching, and it requires executive ownership.
- The paper closes with a measurement framework built on return on learning investment and a program checklist, in the same spirit as the Pre-Implementation Checklist in [Intelligent Business](/papers/intelligent-business).

## Introduction

Every AI integration eventually produces a list of roles whose routine work the new systems will absorb, and every organization holding that list faces the same decision: eliminate the headcount or redirect it. The elimination path is well documented and it is expensive. I have written about that evidence at length in [The $1.27 Problem](/notebook/the-1-27-problem), and the whitepaper this document extends, [Intelligent Business: A Modular Approach to AI Integration](/papers/intelligent-business), makes workforce planning one of its core preparation requirements.

What that earlier work does not provide is the operating manual. Knowing that retraining outperforms displacement does not tell a leadership team where to look for redeployment opportunities, how to structure the program, or how to know whether it is working. Those are the questions this paper answers. The evidence base is a combination of large-sample survey research on reskilling returns, the World Economic Forum's labor projections, and four corporate programs, at AT&T, Amazon, IKEA's Ingka Group, and Shell, that are examined here for their design decisions rather than their headlines.

## The Economics of Displacement

The motivation can be stated in three findings, each from a different research organization, all pointing the same direction.

Workforce analytics firm Orgvue calculated the full cost of AI-driven layoffs, including severance, lost productivity, the collapse of institutional knowledge, and the recruitment fees to rebuild, and found that companies spend approximately [$1.27 for every $1 theoretically saved](https://www.forbes.com/councils/forbestechcouncil/2026/04/24/why-companies-regret-laying-off-workers-for-ai/). Forrester's Predictions 2026 research found that [55% of employers who restructured their workforce for AI now regret the decision](https://hrexecutive.com/the-ai-layoff-trap-why-half-will-be-quietly-rehired/). And Gartner predicts that [50% of companies that cut customer service staff citing AI will be rehiring for similar functions by 2027](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2026-02-03-gartner-predicts-half-of-companies-that-cut-customer-service-staff-due-to-ai-will-rehire-by-2027), which converts the regret from a survey response into a staffing forecast.

The mechanism behind these numbers is that routine roles carry more than routine output. They hold institutional knowledge, they provide the oversight that catches AI errors on hard cases, and they form the apprenticeship pipeline that produces senior staff, an argument developed in full in [Entry-Level Jobs Are Your AI Strategy](/notebook/entry-level-jobs-are-your-ai-strategy). On the other side of the ledger, research from the National Bureau of Economic Research and Harvard University found that [displaced workers who undergo retraining experience consistently positive earnings returns](https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2025/09/ai-took-your-job-can-retraining-help/), which means the alternative to displacement is not charity. It is an investment with a measurable yield.

That is the motivation, and it is deliberately brief, because the harder problem is what a leadership team convinced by these numbers should actually do. The first input to that decision is the scale of the change coming regardless of what any single company decides.

## The Macro Context

The World Economic Forum's [Future of Jobs Report 2025](https://reports.weforum.org/docs/WEF_Future_of_Jobs_Report_2025.pdf), built on a survey of over a thousand global employers, projects that 39% of workers' core skills will change by 2030. Translated into headcount, the report estimates that of every 100 workers, 59 will need training by 2030: 29 can be upskilled within their current role, 19 will need to be reskilled and redeployed into a different role, and 11 are unlikely to receive the training they need at all. Those 19 redeployments per 100 employees are the population this paper is about.

The same report frames both the pressure and the opportunity. Employers named skills gaps their single largest transformation barrier, cited by 63% of respondents, ahead of every technology and regulatory obstacle on the list, and 85% of employers plan to prioritize upskilling their workforce in response. At the labor-market level, the report projects 170 million new roles created against 92 million displaced by 2030, a net gain of 78 million jobs. The aggregate numbers are reassuring, but no individual organization experiences an aggregate. Whether your displaced workers land in your new roles or someone else's depends entirely on whether you can see the match before they leave. Finding that match is a discoverable, repeatable process, and it is where the operational work begins.

## Finding the Opportunities

### Inventory tasks, not roles

The single most common analytical error in workforce planning is evaluating automation exposure at the level of the job title. Roles do not automate; tasks do. Any given job is a bundle of tasks with entirely different exposure profiles, and a role that looks 80% automatable on a org chart usually contains the 20% that was the reason the role existed. The full argument for task-level decision-making is in [The Automation-Augmentation Paradox](/notebook/the-automation-augmentation-paradox); what matters here is the practice.

A **task inventory** is a structured decomposition of each affected role into its constituent tasks, built with the people who do the work rather than inferred from job descriptions, because job descriptions describe the role as designed and the inventory needs the role as performed. For each task, record its volume, its inputs and outputs, whether its output can be verified against a system of record, and the cost of an individual failure. Two profiles emerge from that data. A task is a genuine automation candidate when it is routine and high-volume, verifiable against a trusted source, and cheap to fail at or easy to catch. A task belongs with a person when it requires context no database holds, carries accountability that must trace to a named human, involves relationships, or fails expensively.

The inventory does two jobs at once. It scopes the automation honestly, and it produces the first artifact of the redeployment analysis: a precise picture of what the affected people actually know how to do, task by task, rather than a job title that compresses all of it into two words.

### Look where the automation stopped

The second method is the one Ingka Group used, and it deserves to be named as a general technique rather than remembered as a corporate anecdote. When Ingka's chatbot Billie was [resolving roughly 47% of incoming customer queries](https://www.cio.com/article/4180896/how-ikea-turned-a-chatbot-with-13-million-users-into-a-business-worth-1-3-million.html), the company's decisive move was to analyze the 53% it could not resolve. Inside that residue was a pattern: customers were not asking whether the sofa was in stock, a question a bot answers well, but whether it would suit their living room, a question that wants human judgment and taste. That unmet demand became a remote interior design service staffed by retrained call center workers, and it generated [€1.3 billion in its first full year](https://www.reuters.com/technology/ikea-bets-remote-interior-design-ai-changes-sales-strategy-2023-06-13/).

The general form of the technique: every automation produces a residue of work it could not absorb, and that residue is a free, continuously updated market research report on what your customers and processes need from human beings. Exception queues, escalation logs, and the categories of tickets the model routes to a person are not operational noise to be minimized. They are the highest-signal data your organization holds about where augmented human work is worth more than it costs. Reviewing that residue should be a standing agenda item from the first month of any automation deployment, because this is also the mechanism the research predicts: automating one task reshapes the tasks around it, creating exception handling, review, and judgment work that did not previously exist in that form. The redeployment destination is frequently created by the same project that created the displacement, and organizations that never examine the residue never notice.

### Map skills adjacency

The third method connects the people to the destinations. **Skills adjacency** is the observation that most role transitions are short moves rather than leaps: the displaced worker already holds most of the skills the destination role requires, and the training gap is the difference, not the whole. Ingka's call center workers already possessed the scarce half of the design consultant role, which is product knowledge, customer conversation, and patience with ambiguity. The training added spatial design and the toolset. A program that had scored those workers by job title would have called them customer service staff and looked outside for designers.

The practical implementation is a **skills-to-role map**: for each destination role, an explicit list of required skills; for each affected worker, an inventory of held skills drawn from the task analysis above; and a gap computation between them. AT&T built exactly this at enterprise scale. Its Career Intelligence portal showed every employee [each role's required skills, its salary range, and whether the company projected the role to grow or shrink](https://www.chieflearningofficer.com/2019/06/18/a-fixed-gaze-toward-the-future/), with curricula mapped to roughly 3,000 jobs, so any employee could see the distance between where they stood and any role in the company, and precisely which training closed it. The transparency matters as much as the mapping, and it returns in the program design section below.

### Know your internal supply

The fourth method points inward. Most organizations maintain meticulous inventories of laptops and licenses and no inventory at all of what their people can do. A warehouse that does not know its own stock buys duplicates of parts it already holds, and an organization without a skills inventory does the equivalent in the labor market: it hires externally, at a premium and with a lengthy ramp, for capabilities it already employs under a different job title. McKinsey's research on skill transformations found that organizations are typically strongest at exactly these analytical steps, [demand assessment, supply assessment, and gap analysis](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/people-and-organizational-performance/our-insights/building-workforce-skills-at-scale-to-thrive-during-and-after-the-covid-19-crisis), which is encouraging, because it means the analysis is tractable, and damning, because it means most failures happen after the analysis, in execution.

### The build side: your AI program is a destination too

The fifth method is the one Shell demonstrated, and it is the one most organizations overlook because they are looking only at the customer-facing side of the business. Shell's reskilling program did not begin with displaced workers in search of a destination. It began with [a mismatch between the company's AI project pipeline and the data scientists available to deliver it](https://www.cnbc.com/2020/04/03/royal-dutch-shell-reskills-workers-in-ai-part-of-energy-transition.html), which is a demand problem, found on the build side of the ledger. The answer was a [voluntary program with Udacity](https://www.udacity.com/blog/announcing-our-newest-collaboration-with-shell/) open to petroleum engineers, chemists, and geophysicists, and it produced more than 800 citizen data scientists working alongside roughly 200 dedicated ones.

The generalizable point is that an AI integration program generates its own new labor demand: data product owners, exception reviewers, model output verifiers, knowledge graph maintainers, workflow designers. The architecture in [Intelligent Business](/papers/intelligent-business) names these functions explicitly, and every one of them is performed better by someone who already understands your business than by an external hire who understands only the technology. Before searching the market for AI talent, inventory the roles your own AI program will create and treat them as redeployment destinations.

These five methods produce a list of matched opportunities: displaced task capacity on one side, destination roles on the other, and a computed training gap between them. A list is not a program. Turning one into the other is where most of the investment is won or lost.

## Running the Program

The largest body of evidence on reskilling program design comes from a BCG Henderson Institute and Harvard Business School survey of [1,215 US companies running reskilling programs](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/five-ways-to-make-the-most-of-reskilling-investments). The headline finding is sobering: only 40% of those companies reported a positive **return on learning investment (ROLI)**, meaning the majority of corporate reskilling spend does not pay for itself. The same survey identifies what the successful minority did differently, and its findings converge with BCG's [Digital Reskilling Lab interviews across roughly 40 organizations](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2023/reskilling-workforce-for-future) and McKinsey's skill-transformation research. Six design principles fall out of that evidence.

**Own it at the executive level; do not silo it in the learning department.** The Digital Reskilling Lab's central finding is that reskilling fails when it is treated as a corporate learning function, because a redeployment program is not a training program with extra steps. It is a change-management exercise that spans workforce supply and demand analysis, manager training, on-the-job learning, and job matching, and no learning department controls those levers. The department that owns courseware cannot reassign a worker, redesign a role, or compel a hiring manager to interview internal candidates. McKinsey's research quantifies what completeness is worth: organizations implementing all nine of its identified practices for skill transformations report [close to a 100% success rate, roughly 2.5 times the rate of those missing even one](https://www.mckinsey.com/capabilities/people-and-organizational-performance/our-insights/building-workforce-skills-at-scale-to-thrive-during-and-after-the-covid-19-crisis). Partial programs do not deliver partial results. They deliver failed ones at a discount.

**Pilot before you scale.** In the BCG and Harvard survey, companies that ran a pilot phase for more than a year before scaling achieved positive ROLI 61% of the time, against 26% for those that scaled immediately. The pilot's function is the same as the pilot workflow in the whitepaper's Where to Begin sequence: it exercises the full path, from task inventory through training through actual redeployment into an actual role, on a population small enough to instrument and correct. A pilot that only tests whether people complete the coursework has tested the cheap half of the program.

**Make it opt-in.** The same survey found opt-in programs roughly 1.5 times more likely to yield positive ROLI than mandated ones. The result should not surprise anyone who has sat through mandated training. Adult retraining is effortful, and effort follows motivation that a mandate cannot manufacture. Both Shell and AT&T ran voluntary programs, and both saw participation rates that mandates rarely achieve; more than [180,000 of AT&T's 203,000 employees](https://www.chieflearningofficer.com/2019/06/18/a-fixed-gaze-toward-the-future/) participated in Future Ready. Volunteering is a function of what the program credibly offers, which is the next principle.

**Be transparent about destinations.** People volunteer for retraining when they can see where it leads, and skepticism about vague promises of future opportunity is rational, because employees have watched restructurings before. AT&T's Career Intelligence portal is the reference implementation: every role's required skills, its salary range, and the company's own projection of whether that role would grow or shrink, published internally for every employee to see. That last element deserves emphasis. AT&T told its people which jobs it expected to eliminate, and paired the warning with a visible, funded path to the jobs it expected to grow. Transparency about the destination converts a threat into an offer.

**Train during paid hours, at company expense.** Shell's program ran [project-based coursework during working hours at company expense](https://www.cnbc.com/2020/04/03/royal-dutch-shell-reskills-workers-in-ai-part-of-energy-transition.html). Amazon's Career Choice program [pays tuition up front rather than reimbursing it](https://press.aboutamazon.com/2021/9/amazon-to-pay-full-college-tuition-for-its-front-line-employees-as-part-of-1.2-billion-investment-in-education-and-skills-training-for-u-s-workforce), with eligibility beginning at 90 days of employment and classrooms located on site. These are not generosity signals. They are participation engineering. A program funded by reimbursement selects for employees who can float tuition, and a program run on personal time selects for employees with spare evenings, which systematically excludes the front-line workers redeployment programs exist to serve. Every design choice that moves cost or friction from the company to the worker narrows the funnel at its widest point.

**Support the transition, not just the training.** The BCG and Harvard research identifies transition support as the stage where returns are most commonly lost: companies fund the coursework and then fail to manage the actual move into the new role. A completed curriculum without a role transition is a relocation package without a house. Closing that gap is concrete work: internal hiring processes that treat program completers as qualified candidates rather than external-equivalent applicants, managers who are prepared to receive and ramp redeployed workers, and staffing plans that hold destination roles open for the program's graduates. AT&T's numbers show what a closed loop looks like: in a recent year, [roughly half of the company's 45,000 open roles were filled internally, and internal applicants who had completed training were 50% more likely to land the new job](https://www.cnbc.com/2018/03/13/atts-1-billion-gambit-retraining-nearly-half-its-workforce.html). Training fed hiring, and employees could see it feeding hiring, which fed the next cohort's motivation to volunteer.

## Case Studies as Design Lessons

Each of the four programs referenced above teaches one design lesson best, and reading them as a set shows the same architecture from four angles.

**AT&T Future Ready: transparency at scale.** Facing a network business whose skill requirements were shifting wholesale, AT&T committed [$1 billion over multiple years](https://www.cnbc.com/2018/03/13/atts-1-billion-gambit-retraining-nearly-half-its-workforce.html) to retraining rather than replacement. The design lesson is the information architecture: the Career Intelligence portal mapped curricula to roughly 3,000 jobs and showed every employee every role's skills, pay, and growth projection. The scale of participation, more than 180,000 of 203,000 employees, and the internal fill rate of roughly 50% of 45,000 open roles were consequences of that transparency, not of the budget. A company one-hundredth of AT&T's size can replicate the design with a spreadsheet, because what employees responded to was not the portal's engineering but the honesty of its projections.

**Amazon Upskilling 2025: remove the friction.** Amazon's [$1.2 billion commitment to train 300,000 employees](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/workplace/upskilling-2025) teaches the participation-engineering lesson: tuition paid up front, eligibility after 90 days, classrooms inside the buildings where people already work. The outcome data comes from a Gallup study conducted with Amazon, which found that workers completing upskilling programs saw an [average salary increase of 8.6%, with 75% of participants reporting career advancement](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/workplace/upskilling-2025). Those figures matter for program design because they are the numbers that recruit the next cohort. A reskilling program's most persuasive marketing is the verifiable outcomes of its graduates.

**IKEA/Ingka Billie: the opportunity is in the residue.** The Billie sequence, [47% of queries resolved and roughly €13 million saved](https://www.cio.com/article/4180896/how-ikea-turned-a-chatbot-with-13-million-users-into-a-business-worth-1-3-million.html), then 8,500 call center workers retrained as remote design consultants, then [€1.3 billion in first-full-year sales with a 10% revenue target by 2028](https://www.reuters.com/technology/ikea-bets-remote-interior-design-ai-changes-sales-strategy-2023-06-13/), is told in full in [The $1.27 Problem](/notebook/the-1-27-problem). The design lesson belongs here: the redeployment destination was discovered by analyzing what the automation could not do. Ingka treated the unresolved 53% of queries as evidence of unmet demand, found a service inside it, and staffed that service with the people whose product knowledge the automation could not replicate. The savings and the new revenue came from the same project, and the second figure is two orders of magnitude larger than the first.

**Shell/Udacity: build-side demand, voluntary supply.** Shell's program teaches two lessons at once. The opportunity was found on the build side, in the gap between the AI project pipeline and available data science capacity, and the program design was voluntary, project-based, run during working hours, and open to the domain experts, petroleum engineers, chemists, and geophysicists, whose business knowledge made them better AI practitioners than external hires would have been. The result, [more than 800 citizen data scientists alongside 200 dedicated ones](https://www.udacity.com/blog/announcing-our-newest-collaboration-with-shell/), meant Shell manufactured the scarcest talent in the market from people who already knew the business, instead of bidding for it against everyone else.

Four programs, one pattern: an honest map of where the work is going, a funded and frictionless path for volunteers to follow it, and a real role waiting at the end. What remains is knowing whether it worked.

## Measurement

The discipline here is the same one the whitepaper's Sustainable Economics principle applies to compute: measure the whole cost of the workflow against the quality of the outcome, and let the measurement decide what expands. A reskilling program has a whole cost that includes training content, paid training hours, program staff, and the temporary productivity dip during transition, and it has outcomes that are countable. The BCG and Harvard survey's ROLI framing makes the standard explicit: a program returns its investment or it does not, and only [40% currently do](https://www.bcg.com/publications/2025/five-ways-to-make-the-most-of-reskilling-investments), so measurement is not administrative overhead. It is how a program earns its next budget cycle.

Track four categories:

1. **Pipeline metrics.** Enrollment rate among eligible employees, completion rate, and time to completion. These are the earliest signals and the least meaningful on their own; a program can have excellent completion rates and zero redeployments.
2. **Transition metrics.** The measures that BCG's research identifies as the commonly missing link: the share of program completers actually placed into destination roles, time from completion to placement, and the internal fill rate for destination roles. AT&T's roughly 50% internal fill rate and the 50% placement advantage for training completers are the benchmark shapes here.
3. **Value metrics.** Cost per successful redeployment compared against the fully loaded cost of the alternative, which is severance for the displaced worker plus recruitment, onboarding, and ramp time for an external hire, priced honestly using the same accounting that produces the [$1.27 figure](https://www.forbes.com/councils/forbestechcouncil/2026/04/24/why-companies-regret-laying-off-workers-for-ai/). Where the destination roles generate revenue, as Ingka's did, attribute it; a redeployment program that feeds a revenue line is an investment case, not a cost center.
4. **Durability metrics.** Retention of redeployed workers at twelve and twenty-four months, their performance in the destination roles, and, following the Gallup and Amazon precedent, [participant salary progression](https://www.aboutamazon.com/news/workplace/upskilling-2025). Redeployment that does not hold is displacement on a delay.

Review the measurements on the same cadence as the automation program they accompany, because the task landscape moves. The allocation of work between machines and people is never final, a point argued in [The Automation-Augmentation Paradox](/notebook/the-automation-augmentation-paradox), and a redeployment program that ran once and closed has answered last year's task inventory.

## Program Checklist

- **Task inventory:** Decompose every affected role into tasks, built with the people who perform them, recording volume, verifiability, and cost of failure for each.
- **Task allocation:** Sort tasks against the automation and augmentation profiles, and schedule the date on which the sorting will be repeated.
- **Residue review:** Establish a standing review of what the automation cannot do, including exception queues, escalations, and unresolved-query categories, as a source of new augmented roles.
- **Build-side demand:** Inventory the roles your own AI program creates, including data ownership, verification, and exception review, and list them as redeployment destinations.
- **Skills supply map:** Build the internal inventory of held skills and compute the adjacency gap between affected workers and destination roles.
- **Skills-to-role transparency:** Publish destination roles with required skills, compensation, and growth projections where every eligible employee can see them.
- **Executive ownership:** Place the program under an executive owner with authority over staffing and role design, with the learning function as a supplier rather than the owner.
- **Pilot:** Run one cohort end to end, through placement into real roles, before scaling, and hold the pilot open long enough to observe transitions rather than completions.
- **Opt-in enrollment:** Make participation voluntary and recruit with the pilot cohort's verifiable outcomes.
- **Paid, funded, frictionless:** Deliver training during working hours at company expense, with costs paid up front rather than reimbursed.
- **Transition support:** Prepare receiving managers, hold destination roles for program completers, and treat completion as a qualification in internal hiring.
- **Measurement:** Instrument pipeline, transition, value, and durability metrics from the first cohort, and report them against the displacement alternative's full cost.

## Conclusion

The displacement evidence gave this paper its opening, but the operational evidence carries its argument. Reskilling programs are not uniformly good investments; most currently fail to return their cost. The ones that pay share a discoverable design, which is executive-owned, piloted, voluntary, transparent about destinations, funded on company time, and managed all the way through the transition into a real role. The opportunities they redeploy people into are equally discoverable, sitting in the task inventories, the exception queues, the skills adjacencies, and the staffing needs of the AI program itself.

None of this requires AT&T's budget. It requires the sequence: inventory the tasks, map the skills, publish the destinations, pilot one cohort, measure the result. For an organization beginning its AI integration, the workforce program belongs in the same planning cycle as the architecture, staffed and scheduled alongside it, and the whitepaper's guidance applies to both. The first step is a meeting, [not a purchase order](/notebook/the-first-step-is-a-meeting), and the reskilling assessment belongs on that meeting's agenda before the first role is cut.
