## Executive Summary

- Regulation has caught up with automated decision-making. The EU AI Act requires high-risk AI systems to [record events automatically over their lifetime](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-12), and most enterprise systems cannot satisfy the question that requirement exists to answer: what did the system know at the moment it acted?
- Conventional databases store current state. When a record is corrected, the prior value is gone, and with it goes the ability to reconstruct any decision that depended on it.
- The established remedy is **bitemporal** modeling, which records both when a fact was true in the world and when the system learned it. The approach has been part of the SQL standard since 2011 and is the foundation of modern temporal databases.
- Time alone is not enough. A decision depends on relationships between facts, so the natural shape of a decision's context is a subgraph, and the natural store for it is a temporal knowledge graph with provenance on every edge.
- Paired with immutable logs and replayable queue messages, the temporal graph converts compliance from quarterly evidence assembly into a query against live infrastructure. Surveyed organizations report [83% experiencing delays from manual compliance work](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20260120755843/en/2026-State-of-CCM-Report-Resource-Constraints-Drive-85-of-Organizations-to-Rethink-Traditional-GRC-Approaches); this architecture removes the assembly step entirely.
- This paper is the rigorous treatment of Layer 5 of the [Intelligent Business framework](/papers/intelligent-business), grounded in SQL:2011, W3C PROV, and the emerging harmonized standards under the EU AI Act. It closes with a build-versus-buy landscape and a concrete starting sequence.

## The Audit Problem for AI Systems

An auditor examining an automated decision asks a question that sounds simple: on what basis did the system act? For a decision made by a human, the answer lives in email threads, meeting notes, and testimony. For a decision made by software against business data, the answer must come from the data itself, and here most organizations discover an uncomfortable fact about their infrastructure. Their systems record what is true now. The decision happened then.

Consider a concrete case. In March, an agent approves a customer order against a credit limit. In May, the finance team corrects that customer's record, because the March limit had been entered wrong. In September, an auditor asks why the order was approved. The current database returns the corrected limit, which makes the March decision look either wrong or inexplicable. The value the agent actually saw no longer exists anywhere, because the correction overwrote it. The organization is now reconstructing the past from backups, log fragments, and the memory of whoever was involved, which is the posture I described in [Compliance as a Byproduct](/notebook/compliance-as-a-byproduct) and the posture the survey data confirms is normal: RegScale's 2026 State of Continuous Controls Monitoring report, covering 253 information security leaders at organizations above 1,000 employees, found [53% dedicating the equivalent of a full-time employee to evidence collection](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20260120755843/en/2026-State-of-CCM-Report-Resource-Constraints-Drive-85-of-Organizations-to-Rethink-Traditional-GRC-Approaches) and only 28% monitoring controls continuously.

AI systems make this failure mode both more frequent and more consequential. Agents act faster and more often than the humans an evidence process was designed around, and each action consumes a snapshot of business state that the underlying systems will silently revise. A quarter later, the states those actions depended on have been corrected, enriched, and overwritten thousands of times. Every overwrite destroys evidence.

The question "what did we know when we acted" therefore needs to be answerable by design rather than by archaeology. Answering it by design requires infrastructure that treats time as a first-class dimension of data, and that discipline has a name and a standards lineage.

## Temporal Foundations

Temporal data management distinguishes two independent timelines, and the distinction carries this entire paper.

**Valid time** is when a fact was true in the world. A customer's credit limit was 50,000 from January through June regardless of what any database said during those months. **Transaction time**, also called system time, is when the system learned the fact, meaning when the record was written. The two timelines routinely disagree. A backdated contract has a valid time earlier than its transaction time. A data-entry error corrected in May has a transaction-time history that the world's actual history never contained. A system that records both timelines for every fact is bitemporal, and bitemporality is what makes decision reconstruction possible, because it lets you ask two different questions that current-state databases collapse into one: what was actually true on March 12th, and what did we believe on March 12th?

This is old, settled engineering rather than a research frontier. The [SQL:2011 standard introduced bitemporal primitives](https://docs.xtdb.com/about/time-in-xtdb.html) into the relational mainstream, with application-time periods carrying valid time and system-versioned tables carrying transaction time. Purpose-built temporal databases have carried the model further; XTDB, one of the more rigorous implementations, treats every write as an append to an immutable bitemporal history rather than a mutation of current state. It is worth noting the distinction has teeth even among temporal systems: [Datomic, a well-known immutable database, is unitemporal](https://xtdb.com/blog/building-a-bitemp-index-2-resolution), recording transaction time only, which preserves the system's history but cannot represent when facts were true in the world independently of when they were recorded.

The XTDB documentation offers the exact scenario that motivates this paper. When an auditor challenges a decision made at time T3, the organization must [justify it against the data available at system-time T3](https://xtdb.com/blog/building-a-bitemp-index-2-resolution), including whatever errors that data contained, rather than against the corrected data that exists today. The March order approval from the previous section is precisely this case. In a bitemporal store the question is a query with two timestamp parameters. In a current-state store it is a forensic project.

Accountants solved the underlying problem centuries before databases existed, and their solution is the right mental model. A ledger is append-only. When an entry is wrong, the bookkeeper does not erase it; a correcting entry is added, dated the day the error was found, and the original stays on the page. Anyone reviewing the books can therefore see both the mistake and its correction, each in its proper place in time, and can reconstruct exactly what the books said on any past date. Bitemporal storage is that discipline applied to operational data: corrections are new facts about old periods, and nothing is ever silently rewritten.

Bitemporality tells you how to store individual facts. A decision, however, rarely rests on an individual fact, and the structure connecting the facts is where the next requirement comes from.

## Why the Graph

Return to the March order approval and enumerate what the agent actually consulted: the customer record, the credit limit on it, the outstanding invoices that counted against the limit, the payment terms on the governing contract, and the policy that defined the approval threshold. The decision did not depend on five isolated values. It depended on the relationships among them, because the invoices matter only as they attach to this customer, and the threshold matters only as the contract selects it. The context of the decision is a connected structure. It is, precisely, a subgraph.

This is why the audit store in my architecture is a knowledge graph rather than a log pipeline. I made the general case for graphs in [Knowledge Graphs Explained](/notebook/knowledge-graphs-explained): they store relationships with the same seriousness that conventional databases store facts, and they make traversal the cheap operation. For audit purposes that property is decisive. Reconstructing a decision from log lines means grepping timestamped strings across services and mentally rebuilding the connections between them, and every inference the reviewer makes is a place the reconstruction can be wrong. Reconstructing it from a temporal graph means retrieving the decision's subgraph as of the decision's moment, with the connections stored rather than inferred. The evidence arrives already assembled, in the shape the decision actually had.

The pattern is no longer theoretical, because AI agent memory has forced it into production tooling. [Graphiti](https://github.com/getzep/graphiti), the open-source temporal graph framework underlying Zep's agent memory platform, implements exactly the discipline this paper argues for: every edge carries bi-temporal metadata, and when the world changes, facts are [invalidated rather than deleted](https://www.getzep.com/ai-agents/temporal-knowledge-graph/), so the graph accumulates history instead of overwriting it. Ask Graphiti what was true last March and it answers from the edges valid last March. Its provenance model is equally relevant: facts trace back to the episodes, meaning the source messages and documents, from which they were extracted, so every derived assertion in the graph can be walked back to the raw data that produced it. The architecture is documented in Zep's [technical paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.13956). That the agent-memory community independently converged on bitemporal graphs with provenance is worth pausing on, because they were solving for agent recall quality and arrived at audit infrastructure anyway. The requirements are the same requirement. An agent that needs to know what it knew, and an auditor who needs to know what the agent knew, are served by one store.

A temporal graph with provenance is therefore the technically sufficient answer to the reconstruction question. Whether your organization is obligated to answer that question is no longer a matter of engineering taste.

## The Regulatory Case

The EU AI Act is the first major regulation to make decision reconstruction a legal requirement rather than a best practice. [Article 12](https://ai-act-service-desk.ec.europa.eu/en/ai-act/article-12) requires that high-risk AI systems technically allow the automatic recording of events over the lifetime of the system, with logging capabilities that ensure traceability appropriate to the system's intended purpose. [Article 19 requires providers to retain those automatically generated logs](https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2026/04/16/eu-ai-act-logging-requirements/) for at least six months, with longer periods where other law demands them.

Two words in Article 12 do the heavy lifting, as the practical analyses have noted: [automatic and lifetime](https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2026/04/16/eu-ai-act-logging-requirements/). Automatic rules out logging that depends on operator discipline, because a record produced only when someone remembers to produce it is not a capability of the system. Lifetime rules out logging bolted on for the audit season. Together they describe recording as a structural property of the system, which is the same property the Intelligent Business framework demands for different reasons: every action travels a governed path, and the path records.

The same analysis makes a point that should shape procurement decisions now: [logs that can be silently altered have zero evidentiary value](https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2026/04/16/eu-ai-act-logging-requirements/). A mutable audit trail proves nothing to a skeptical third party, because the trail itself cannot demonstrate that it reflects what happened rather than what someone later preferred. Immutability is not a nice-to-have property of audit storage. It is the property that makes the storage evidence.

Organizations waiting for a finalized technical standard before acting should understand the current state honestly. No harmonized standard for AI Act logging is finished; [prEN 18229-1 and ISO/IEC DIS 24970 are both still in draft](https://www.helpnetsecurity.com/2026/04/16/eu-ai-act-logging-requirements/). That is an argument for building on the standards that do exist and have existed for years, which is what the temporal foundations above provide on the storage side. On the semantics side, meaning what the records themselves should say, there is likewise a mature standard, and it deserves more attention from enterprise architects than it gets.

## Provenance Standards

The W3C published [PROV-DM](https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-dm/), its provenance data model, as a recommendation in 2013, together with [PROV-O](https://www.w3.org/TR/prov-o/), the OWL ontology that expresses the model for linked-data systems. **Provenance**, in the standard's usage, is information about the entities, activities, and people involved in producing a piece of data, which can be used to assess its quality, reliability, or trustworthiness. That is an exact statement of what an AI audit record must contain.

The model is compact enough to state in a paragraph. There are three node types: an **Entity** is a thing, such as a document, a record, or a derived fact; an **Activity** is something that occurs over time and acts on entities; an **Agent** is something that bears responsibility for an activity, whether a person, an organization, or a piece of software. The relations connect them: an activity *used* entities and other entities *were generated by* it, an entity *was derived from* another entity, and an activity *was associated with* the agent responsible for it. Map the vocabulary onto the March approval and the fit is immediate. The approval decision is an entity generated by an approval activity; the activity used the customer record, the invoices, and the contract terms; each of those entities was derived from upstream source systems; and the activity was associated with the software agent that executed it, which in my framework is itself bound to the named human operator who authorized it.

The reason to adopt the standard vocabulary rather than inventing an in-house one is interchange. Your provenance records will eventually be read by parties who did not build your system: external auditors, regulators, customers running vendor assessments, and your own staff five years from now. A W3C-standard provenance graph is self-describing to all of them. It also composes naturally with the knowledge graph itself, since PROV-O is an ontology and its terms become edge types in the same store that holds the business facts. The provenance is not a parallel record about the graph. It is part of the graph.

The pieces are now on the table: bitemporal storage, graph structure, provenance semantics, and a regulatory requirement they jointly satisfy. What remains is to show how they compose into a running system.

## Architecture: Layer 5 and the Chain of Custody

In the [Intelligent Business framework](/papers/intelligent-business), Layer 5 is the knowledge, long temporal, and audit store: durable memory built on temporal graphs and read-only log storage. This paper has so far justified its internal design. Its role in the running system comes from how it composes with the layers around it, and with one property of Layer 3 in particular.

The framework's messaging backbone never destroys a message. Every task ever dispatched persists with its complete contents, including routing labels, instructions, payload data, and the authorization it carried, and persisted messages can be replayed through the same execution path in an isolated environment. I covered the compliance value of replay in [Compliance as a Byproduct](/notebook/compliance-as-a-byproduct) and its incident-response value in [the data poisoning entry](/notebook/what-data-poisoning-means-for-your-business), where replay is how an organization measures the blast radius of a flawed input after correcting it. The three pieces of core machinery are introduced in the [architecture series overview](/notebook/queue-workers-knowledge-graph).

Composed, the three storage disciplines answer the three questions any serious audit asks, and it is worth stating the division of labor plainly.

The immutable log answers whether it happened. Every event, permission check, and write is appended to storage that workers and agents cannot alter, which is the property the evidentiary-value argument above requires.

The temporal knowledge graph answers what was known. Given a decision's timestamp, a bitemporal query retrieves the subgraph of business state as the system knew it at that moment, with PROV edges connecting the decision to the facts it used, the sources those facts derived from, and the agent and authorizing human responsible.

The replayable message answers what was done with what was known. When interpretation of the record is not enough, the original task is re-executed against the reconstructed state and observed directly.

The chain of custody framing I introduced in the notebook treatment applies with full force here, and the temporal graph is what completes it. A chain of custody for physical evidence documents who collected an exhibit, who handled it, and how it moved. The immutable log provides the handling record for every automated action, but a handling record alone cannot establish what the actor perceived at the time of action, and that is the link the bitemporal graph supplies. Together they give every automated decision what a courtroom demands of an exhibit: an unbroken, tamper-evident account from origin to outcome, produced by a mechanism the actors cannot bypass, queryable on demand rather than assembled under deadline. The RegScale finding that [83% of organizations suffer moderate or major delays from manual compliance work](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20260120755843/en/2026-State-of-CCM-Report-Resource-Constraints-Drive-85-of-Organizations-to-Rethink-Traditional-GRC-Approaches) is a measurement of exactly the assembly step this composition eliminates.

## Practitioner Guidance

I run graph knowledge stores in my own work, and this section reflects that experience rather than a vendor survey. The honest summary is that the technology is available at every budget, and the difficulty concentrates in places the procurement process does not usually look.

The build-versus-buy landscape follows the same split as the framework's [component index](/papers/intelligent-business). On the self-hosted side sit MuninnDB, TrustGraph, modified OpenSearch, Chroma, and pgvector on PostgreSQL; the pgvector option deserves particular mention for organizations with strong PostgreSQL operations, because the relational tooling and operational maturity of PostgreSQL come with it. On the managed side sit Pinecone, Amazon Neptune, Neo4j AuraDB, Google Cloud Spanner Graph, Stardog, Ontotext GraphDB, and Memgraph Cloud. For the temporal semantics specifically, [XTDB](https://docs.xtdb.com/about/time-in-xtdb.html) implements bitemporality natively, and [Graphiti](https://github.com/getzep/graphiti) provides an open-source temporal graph layer designed for agent workloads that can sit atop a general-purpose graph database.

Three tradeoffs belong in any evaluation, and I would rather state them here than have you discover them in month six.

First, most graph databases are not natively bitemporal. Temporality in a general-purpose store is a modeling discipline: validity intervals and recording timestamps on edges, invalidation instead of deletion, and query patterns that filter by both timelines. The discipline is well understood and Graphiti demonstrates it in open source, but it must be designed in from the first edge, because retrofitting temporal semantics onto a graph that has been overwriting itself is reconstruction work of exactly the kind this architecture exists to avoid.

Second, an append-only bitemporal store grows without bound by design, and the queries that make it valuable, meaning as-of traversals across two time dimensions, are more expensive than current-state lookups. Both costs are manageable with tiering, snapshotting, and honest capacity planning, and both will surprise a team that sized the system against a current-state baseline.

Third, and largest, is the maintenance tail I described in [Knowledge Graphs Explained](/notebook/knowledge-graphs-explained). The ontology, the ingestion pipelines, and the provenance discipline are commitments with no end date, and they need named owners. This weighs more, not less, in the audit context, because an audit store that has drifted from its sources is worse than none at all; it returns confident, timestamped, provenance-decorated answers about a past that did not happen. The framework treats ingestion and stewardship as a dedicated layer with accountable owners for precisely this reason.

Weigh the tradeoffs against the baseline honestly, though. The baseline is not zero cost; it is the full-time employee that [53% of surveyed organizations already spend on evidence collection](https://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20260120755843/en/2026-State-of-CCM-Report-Resource-Constraints-Drive-85-of-Organizations-to-Rethink-Traditional-GRC-Approaches), plus the unpriced risk of a regulatory question the organization cannot answer.

## Where to Begin

The path in is incremental, and the first step costs an afternoon.

1. **Run the reconstruction drill.** Pick one automated decision your systems made last quarter and attempt to produce what it knew: the input records as they stood at that moment, the authorization, and the sources the inputs came from. Time the exercise. The result is your current audit posture, measured, and it is the number every later step improves.
2. **Stop destroying history in one dataset.** Choose the governed dataset your automated decisions depend on most and make its writes append-only with recording timestamps, using system-versioned tables if you are in a SQL:2011-capable database. This is transaction time, and it can be adopted without any graph at all.
3. **Model one decision's context in PROV terms.** Take a single workflow and write down its entities, activities, and agents with the used, generated-by, derived-from, and associated-with relations among them. The exercise takes a whiteboard session and produces the ontology seed for everything that follows.
4. **Pilot the temporal graph on that one workflow.** Populate a small store, bitemporal from the first edge, from the append-only dataset and the PROV model, and wire the workflow's decisions to record their context subgraph as they execute.
5. **Re-run the drill and show both numbers to your compliance team.** The distance between the first measurement and the second is the business case, stated in the only currency that survives a budget meeting.

The regulatory deadlines are a reason to start, and they are not the best reason. A system that can always answer what it knew when it acted is a system whose incidents can be investigated, whose agents can be trusted with more, and whose compliance evidence exists before anyone asks. The organizations that reach that posture will have done the unglamorous work early, and the first piece of it is the drill above, this month, on one decision.
