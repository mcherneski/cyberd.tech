Plants are a control-systems problem that happens to be alive. That's the whole appeal.

The itch behind this build is bigger than a houseplant: I'm fascinated by the grow process, and I keep circling one question: if you can hold perfect growing conditions in a box on a desk, what does it take to hold them in a shipping container, a rooftop, a basement in a food desert? Urban farming lives or dies on exactly this kind of automation. So I started at the smallest useful scale.

The chamber itself is a standard fish tank, which turns out to be a great chassis: transparent, sealed enough to hold a climate, cheap, and nobody cries if you drill it. Inside, an Arduino runs the show against a sensor array from Mouser (temperature, humidity, light level, and soil moisture) with grow lights on automated schedules. The 3D printer earns its keep on the unglamorous parts: sensor mounts, light brackets, cable passthroughs, and the little fittings that turn "electronics resting on a tank" into "a machine."

The control loop is the fun part. The Arduino reads the sensors, compares against the target ranges for whatever is growing, and adjusts: lights on schedule, conditions nudged back into band when they drift. The plants don't care how elegant the code is. They are a brutally honest test harness: get the loop wrong and the evidence wilts in front of you.

It's still evolving: more crops, better logging, and eventually pushing the sensor data somewhere I can chart a full grow cycle. The full build log (parts list, wiring, code, and what died along the way) is coming as this one matures.
