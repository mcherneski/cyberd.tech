## Brand model (v3) — the science guy, not the consultancy

You are not selling a service catalog; you are building an audience around **you**. The pitch is: *"Here's me. I'm genuinely competent across a wide range of things, and I like to teach what I've learned."* That is the Bill Nye / MythBusters archetype — a trusted, likeable expert whose credibility comes precisely from ranging across disciplines and explaining them well.

Why this is the right model for you:

- **Range reads as mastery, not dabbling** — *when* it's paired with real rigor and a teacher's clarity. You have both. A person who can speak to enterprise AI governance *and* solder a PCB *and* deploy serverless infra is demonstrating the rarest thing in tech: genuine systems understanding across the whole stack, from electrons to abstractions.
- **The professional work earns trust; the personal work earns a following.** Following is the top of the funnel almost no consultant has. The tinker projects (software, cloud, AI, PCB, mechanical, electrical, 3D printing) are what make people subscribe, share, and come back — and some fraction of that audience is, or reports to, a buyer.
- **A face beats a firm.** The "science guy" brand is a recognizable human. That's why the wordmark puts *your name* under Cyberd, why video matters, and why the tone can be warm and opinionated rather than corporate.

### Personal / Professional as core information architecture

Projects (and eventually tutorials) carry a **Personal** or **Professional** label and are **filterable**. This isn't audience segregation — it's context-setting. Labels let an enterprise CTO enjoy a 3D-printing build as *evidence the architect actually builds*, instead of wondering whether you're serious. The frame removes the only real downside of range.

- **Professional** = client engagements, enterprise architecture, Moxa, the framework. The "why you'd hire or trust me."
- **Personal** = tinker projects across hardware and software. The "why you'd follow me."
- Same production rigor on both. Playful subject, disciplined method (the MythBusters contract). This is what converts range into authority.
- Implemented Jul 3, 2026: `context: personal | professional` on every project, an All / Personal / Professional toggle on the homepage and projects archive, and a labeled badge on each card.

## The core positioning problem

Right now the site says "founder / enterprise tech / AI-native systems" — three fragments with no claim. Visitors (and AI agents) leave without knowing what you *do for them*.

You have an unusual and genuinely rare combination:

1. **Builder's range** — you have personally operated every layer of the stack: IT support, Azure/M365 administration, enterprise architecture, serverless infrastructure across the major cloud providers, blockchain systems, quantum exploration, and now AI/agentic architecture. Most architects have one of these; almost nobody has the whole vertical — and you got it by *tinkering and building*, which is exactly the story to tell.
2. **A codified point of view** — the *Enterprise Intelligence Design* whitepaper is a real framework (zero trust for AI agents, six-layer architecture, human-in-the-loop accountability). That's a differentiator most independent architects never produce.
3. **Proof you live it** — Moxa's infrastructure repos, `moxa-cli` ("one binary, two audiences"), and this very website (AI-readable, serverless, least-privilege) all *demonstrate* the philosophy instead of just asserting it.
4. **A teaching instinct** — the site's job is to give real value away: tutorials, blogs, frameworks, and eventually videos. Education is the top of your funnel *and* the proof of competence.

## Voice & tone

You asked for: professional, kind, knowledgeable, competent — now explicitly **techno-optimist**. Concretely:

- **Optimism about technology, discipline about deployment.** The whitepaper already threads this needle: AI "holds immense promise" *and* needs "a strict foundation, anchored by a set of non-negotiable core principles." Excitement and rigor together are the tone. Never doomerism, never hype.
- **Enablement over replacement.** Use the whitepaper's own principle: "Prioritize Enablement — build AI systems which augment human judgment and capability, not replace. Talent is more difficult to acquire than compute." That last sentence is quotable brand voice; use it verbatim on the site.
- **Plain declarative sentences.** "AI is only as capable as its underlying data." Carry that voice everywhere.
- **Confident, never boastful.** Claims paired with evidence. Prefer "Here's a framework I use" over "I'm an expert in."
- **Generous.** Publish real frameworks, tutorials, and checklists for free. Kindness reads as generosity plus respect for the reader's time.
- **Builder's delight.** It's fine — good, even — for the tinkering to show ("tech and tinkering" is already your X bio). One playful line per page keeps it human; the pinned-tweet energy ("Get in loser, we're saving the world") belongs in social copy, not the enterprise homepage.
- **First person.** "I" for Mike's judgment and history; "Cyberd" for the practice/brand. Avoid corporate "we" unless referring to Moxa's team.

## Audience priorities

1. **The audience/following** — practitioners, tinkerers, and the technically curious who arrive via videos and tutorials. In the science-guy model this is the flywheel; they subscribe, share, and become the top of every other funnel. (Moved to #1 in v3.)
2. **Enterprise/mid-market technology leaders** (CTO, VP Eng, Director of IT) considering AI adoption — the whitepaper audience and highest-value engagements. Many arrive *through* the following.
3. **Founders and small teams** who need fractional architecture/CTO judgment.
4. **Peers, collaborators, and hiring managers** validating credibility.
5. **AI agents** researching you on behalf of any of the above — keep every claim in agent-readable form.

## Wordmark & identity (new in v2)

**Lockup:** the wordmark is **Cyberd**, with **Mike Cherneski** in smaller text directly beneath it. Direction for "modern and techy but not average":

- **Type treatment:** heavy geometric sans or semi-mono for "Cyberd" (the site already loads a strong black weight; a mono-adjacent face like *Space Grotesk*, *JetBrains Mono* at heavy weight, or custom-tracked *Archivo Black* fits the brutalist system). Tight tracking, lowercase "cyberd" or all-caps "CYBERD" both work — lowercase reads more modern.
- **One deliberate glyph move, not five.** Pick a single distinguishing detail so it doesn't look like a default Google-font logo. Candidates:
  - Terminal cursor: `cyberd_` with a blinking (CSS, `prefers-reduced-motion`-aware) underscore — nods to CLI/builder identity.
  - The "d" rendered in the accent color (site already has sky/crimson/yellow accents).
  - A clipped or squared counter in the "y" or "b" for a circuit-like feel.
- **Subline:** `MIKE CHERNESKI` in small caps / mono, letter-spaced wide (0.2em+), muted color, roughly 25–30% of the wordmark height. Optionally `MIKE CHERNESKI · ENTERPRISE ARCHITECT` once the positioning ships.
- **Avoid:** gradients, hexagons, circuit-board clip art, "AI sparkle" icons — the average-looking tech tropes you asked to skip. The brutalist restraint *is* the distinctiveness.
- Implementation target: the site header component and favicon/OG image. Text-based (styled HTML/SVG), not a raster image, so it stays crisp and agent-readable.

## Naming and identity notes

- Use **"Mike Cherneski"** as the person and **"Cyberd"** as the practice — the new lockup literally encodes this. The homepage should introduce both within the first screen.
- Add your face. A professional (or at least deliberate) photo raises conversion for consulting sites significantly; brutalist design + one strong portrait works well.
- Location is an asset, not trivia: "Asheville, NC — working with teams everywhere."
- Unify handles where possible: GitHub `mcherneski` says company "Cyberd", LinkedIn says "Moxa", X says "CTO Hobbyist @ Cyberd". Decide the public story (recommended: "Founder, Cyberd LLC · Co-Founder/CTO, Moxa") and make every profile match. "CTO Hobbyist" is charming but undersells you on the profile enterprise buyers are most likely to check.

## Social profile alignment (worth a deliberate decision)

Your X account is verified, active, and human — but it's mostly replies, and a large share is political commentary on contentious topics. That's entirely your call to keep; it's your account. The brand question is only about **linking**: the website currently offers X as a primary contact surface, which means an enterprise buyer's second click after "AI architecture consultant" may land on heated geopolitical threads.

Options, in order of my recommendation:

1. **Lead with LinkedIn and GitHub on the site; keep X available but de-emphasized.** No change to how you use X.
2. Keep X prominent but start posting original tech content (the tutorial/article roadmap in `04-articles-and-papers.md` gives you ready-made threads), so professional content is what pinned/top-of-profile visitors see.
3. Separate accounts (personal vs. Cyberd). Most effort, usually not worth it at this stage.
